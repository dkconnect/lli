Can a company manipulate numbers to hide debt or distort the financing picture in M&A?

January 22, 2026 [ DOWNLOAD PDF ]
"What if a company undergoing a merger or acquisition manages to manipulate the numbers? For instance, what if they artificially alter the debt-vs-equity mix, misrepresent the cost of financing, or even hide loan obligations entirely? What happens then?"

Short answer: They can TRY but large investment banks, auditors, regulators, and due-diligence teams make it almost impossible for them to succeed.

1. Investment Banks Do Deep Due Diligence

Before advising or valuing a deal, banks like J.P. Morgan, Goldman Sachs, and BlackRock perform extensive checks:

  • Financial Due Diligence: Inspect full audited statements, internal ledgers, and bank loan agreements.
  • Legal Due Diligence: Review legal contracts, liens, collateral, and pending lawsuits.
  • Tax Due Diligence: Scrutinize for hidden tax liabilities.
  • Operational Due Diligence: Verify operations match reported numbers.

2. Companies Cannot Arbitrarily Change Debt vs Equity Mix

Debt and equity are legal obligations that cannot be "hidden" because they create mandatory interest payments, fixed maturity dates, and legal contracts with lenders. These show up in notes to financial statements, cash flow from financing activities, and SEC filings.

3. Off-Balance-Sheet Debt?

New accounting standards (IFRS 16 / ASC 842) and post-Enron regulations require full disclosure of operating leases, Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs), and guarantees. Investment banks specifically look for these to adjust valuations.

4. Manipulation of Financing Costs

Banks don’t rely on management’s projections. They create their own assumptions for interest rates, borrowing capacity, and credit ratings.

Example: If a company says "We can borrow at 6%", the bank will run scenarios at 6% (Management), 7% (Base), and 9% (Downside). The model stays robust even if management lies.

5. Can companies hide loans?

Fraud destroys deals. Banks request bank confirmations, loan schedules, and collateral registers. These cannot be faked without collusion, which is extremely rare in institutional deals.

6. Quality of Earnings (QoE) Reports

Big Four firms (KPMG, PwC, EY, Deloitte) perform QoE audits to expose inflated earnings, hidden working-capital irregularities, and accounting distortions.

7. Why Manipulation Fails in Real M&A

There are simply too many eyes watching: J.P. Morgan, BlackRock, Deloitte, the SEC, and Credit Rating Agencies all study the same deal simultaneously.

8. Example:Enron

Enron tried to hide debt using SPVs. Bankers found red flags, the stock collapsed, and the company was destroyed. This resulted in the Sarbanes-Oxley Act, making modern M&A due diligence incredibly strict.
